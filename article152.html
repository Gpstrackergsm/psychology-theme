Inside Why Nearly All Women in STEM Secretly Feel Like Impostors: What the Brain Tells Us

By Dr. Aris • April 07, 2026

Imposter syndrome is a critical issue in high-performance fields like STEM. Discover the psychological cost of feeling like a 'fraud.'

A striking new study has revealed that nearly all women in graduate-level STEM programs experience imposter syndrome, despite objective evidence of their high intelligence and success. This mindset—attributing success to luck rather than skill—leads to chronic stress, higher burnout rates, and a significantly increased risk of dropping out. Psychologically, it creates a 'threat-focused' state of mind that prevents the individual from actually enjoying their achievements. Overcoming imposterism requires building strong female-centric networks and shifting the narrative from 'perfection' to 'persistence' in scientific communities.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is imposter syndrome a mental illness?

No, it is a psychological pattern or mindset, but it is a major risk factor for clinical anxiety and depression.

Are men affected by imposter syndrome?

Yes, but studies show women, especially in male-dominated fields, experience it at much higher intensities and frequencies.
