Why You Can't Stop Overthinking: The Neuroscience of Mental Spirals

By Dr. Maya Ariston, PhD•April 17, 2026•10 min read
Dr. Maya Ariston, PhD Clinical Psychologist & Editor-in-Chief · Mind & Balance View editorial credentials →

You know you should stop. You've told yourself to stop. But the thoughts keep coming. Here is the truth: you can't think your way out of overthinking — not until you understand the biology running the loop.

The Default Mode Network: Your Brain's Rumination Engine

The Default Mode Network (DMN) is a collection of interconnected brain regions — including the medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, and hippocampus — that activate whenever you are not focused on an external task. It is the brain's "idle mode," and its primary function is self-referential processing: thinking about yourself, your relationships, your past, and your future.

In healthy brains, the DMN switches off when you engage in goal-directed activity and the Task-Positive Network (TPN) takes over. In overthinkers, this handover is broken. The DMN stays "sticky," intruding on conscious experience and flooding the mind with unresolved scenarios, worst-case analyses, and social rehearsals.

Neuroimaging research published in Biological Psychiatry (Hamilton et al., 2015) showed that individuals with depression and anxiety disorders demonstrate abnormally high DMN activity and weakened connectivity between the DMN and prefrontal regulatory regions — meaning the "off switch" is physically impaired.

Key Insight:

Overthinking is not a character flaw — it is a measurable pattern of abnormal DMN activity that can be changed through targeted behavioral and neurological interventions.

Cortisol: The Stress Hormone Feeding the Loop

When the DMN generates a threatening thought — "What if I said the wrong thing?" — the amygdala responds as if the threat is real. It triggers the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis to release cortisol, the body's primary stress hormone. Cortisol's job is to prepare you for action. But in the context of overthinking, there is no action to take. The cortisol pools in your bloodstream, keeping your nervous system in a state of sustained arousal.

High cortisol does three things that worsen overthinking:

  • Enhances threat detection: The amygdala becomes hypersensitive, flagging neutral events as potential threats.
  • Impairs prefrontal function: The rational, regulating prefrontal cortex is suppressed — reducing your ability to "talk yourself down."
  • Strengthens memory encoding: Stressful thoughts and memories are encoded more deeply, making them easier to retrieve and harder to forget.

The Genetics of Anxiety: What Research Actually Says

In a landmark 2019 paper in Molecular Psychiatry, Purves and colleagues analyzed genetic data from over 25,000 individuals and found that chromosome 9 carries variants associated with the development of anxiety disorders. The study concluded that anxiety disorders have a heritability rate of approximately 26% — meaning genetics accounts for roughly one-quarter of the risk.

This also means that 74% of the risk is environmental: your upbringing, trauma history, current stressors, sleep patterns, diet, and social environment. This is profoundly good news. You cannot rewrite your DNA, but you have enormous leverage over the majority of what makes you an overthinker.

The Itch-Scratching Cycle of Rumination

Nick Trenton's "Stop Overthinking" introduces a powerful metaphor: rumination is like scratching an itch. When anxiety generates an uncomfortable thought, the mind instinctively begins analyzing it — trying to "solve" the problem. This analysis produces a small, temporary sense of relief (a dopamine micro-reward), which reinforces the behavior. But like scratching an itch, it doesn't fix the underlying problem. It inflames it.

The formal name for this is metacognitive rumination: thinking about your thoughts, believing that the act of thinking will resolve the anxiety. Research by Adrian Wells at the University of Manchester established that positive beliefs about worry ("If I think about this enough, I'll find an answer") are one of the strongest predictors of chronic worry disorder.

Why Thought Suppression Backfires

The most intuitive strategy — "just stop thinking about it" — is also the least effective. Social psychologist Daniel Wegner's famous "White Bear" experiment (1987) demonstrated that instructing people not to think of a white bear causes them to think about it more frequently than control subjects. He called this the ironic process theory: the very mental monitoring process required to suppress a thought keeps activating the representation of that thought.

This is why clinical treatments focus on acceptance and defusion rather than suppression — acknowledging the thought without engaging in its content, rather than battling it.

The Physical Toll of Chronic Overthinking

Sustained cortisol elevation from chronic rumination creates measurable physical damage:

  • Cardiovascular: Elevated heart rate and blood pressure over time increase cardiovascular disease risk.
  • Immune suppression: Chronic cortisol suppresses the immune system, increasing vulnerability to illness.
  • Sleep disruption: Cortisol's early morning surge (the Cortisol Awakening Response) peaks abnormally early in chronic overthinkers, causing 3 AM waking and nocturnal rumination.
  • Memory impairment: Hippocampal volume — critical for memory formation — is reduced in individuals with chronic anxiety and depression.
  • Digestive issues: The gut-brain axis is directly affected; cortisol disrupts the gut microbiome and exacerbates IBS symptoms.

Neural Plasticity: The Path Out

The single most important finding in modern neuroscience is that the adult brain retains neuroplasticity — the ability to physically restructure itself in response to experience. The rumination circuits that feel hardwired can, with consistent practice, be weakened. New regulatory pathways can be strengthened.

Three interventions with strong neuroimaging evidence for restructuring the overthinking brain:

  1. Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR): An 8-week MBSR program was shown by Sara Lazar at Harvard to significantly reduce gray matter density in the amygdala and increase thickness in the prefrontal cortex — literally growing the "brake" and shrinking the "accelerator."
  2. Aerobic exercise: 30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise 3x/week significantly reduces DMN hyperactivity and increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which promotes the growth of new neurons in the hippocampus.
  3. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): CBT produces measurable changes in prefrontal cortex activity visible on fMRI scans after as few as 12 sessions.

🔑 The Core Takeaway

Overthinking is a biological loop, not a personal failure. The DMN, amygdala, and cortisol are running a script. Understanding the script is the first step to rewriting it — and neuroscience confirms that the rewriting is genuinely possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Default Mode Network?

The DMN is a set of brain regions active during rest and self-reflection. An overactive DMN is the primary driver of rumination and overthinking.

How does cortisol cause overthinking?

Cortisol keeps the amygdala on high alert, lowering the threshold for threat detection and reinforcing anxious thought loops even when no real threat is present.

Is overthinking genetic?

Partly. Research by Purves et al. (2019) found anxiety disorders have a 26% heritability rate, meaning genes play a role but environment accounts for 74% of the risk.
