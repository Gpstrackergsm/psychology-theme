Overcoming Perfectionism: The Art of Being Good Enough

By Mind & Balance Editorial Team • June 8, 2026 • 5 min read

In modern culture, perfectionism is often disguised as a positive trait. We highlight it in job interviews as our "greatest weakness" that is actually a strength. However, clinical psychology paints a very different, far darker picture of perfectionism.

The Illusion of Control

At its core, perfectionism is not about high standards; it is an anxiety-driven mechanism designed to exert control over an unpredictable world. Brene Brown defines perfectionism as a "twenty-ton shield that we lug around, hoping it will keep us from being hurt." If we act perfectly, look perfectly, and work perfectly, the subconscious logic dictates that we can completely avoid judgment, blame, and shame.

But because perfection does not exist, the perfectionist subjects themselves to constant failure. The gap between their impossible expectations and reality creates a perpetual cycle of harsh self-criticism, leading directly to burnout, severe procrastination, and clinical depression.

"Perfectionism is self-destructive simply because there is no such thing as perfect. Perfection is an unattainable goal."

Moving Toward "Optimal" Instead of Perfect

Psychologists recommend shifting your goalpost from perfection to optimization. Perfection says, "This project must have zero flaws, no matter how many sleepless nights it takes." Optimization says, "This project must be delivered by Friday and be highly effective, taking into account my energy limits.

  • Embrace the 80/20 Rule: Recognize that 80% of the value often comes from 20% of the effort. Spending an extra ten hours to polish the final 5% is rarely mathematically sound in the real world.
  • Practice Self-Compassion: When you inevitably fall short of an impossible standard, talk to yourself exactly as you would talk to a dear friend in the same situation. You would never tell a friend they are worthless for making a small error; extend that same grace to yourself.
